Module 1: Introduction to Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and programs from digital attacks. These cyberattacks are usually aimed at accessing, changing, or destroying sensitive information; extorting money from users; or interrupting normal business processes.


The Human Element: Firewalls and encryption are strong, but the easiest way into a system is often through the front door—by tricking a human into handing over the keys.

Module 2: What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of social engineering where an attacker sends a fraudulent message designed to trick a person into revealing sensitive information or deploying malicious software on the victim's infrastructure.


Key Analogy: It's called "phishing" because the attacker throws out "bait" (a fake email) hoping a "fish" (you) will "bite" (click the link).

Module 3: Types of Phishing

  • Email Phishing: Mass emails sent to thousands of people.
  • Spear Phishing: Highly targeted emails addressing you by name, using gathered intelligence.
  • Whaling: Spear phishing aimed at executives (CEOs, CFOs).
  • Smishing: Phishing via SMS/Text messages.
  • Vishing: Voice phishing over phone calls.
  • Clone Phishing: Replicating a legitimate email you previously received, but swapping the safe link for a malicious one.

Module 5: Recognizing Fake Websites

Attackers create pixel-perfect copies of login pages (Microsoft, Google, Banks).


How to check:

  • Check the URL: Is it `microsoft.com` or `microsoft-login-update.com`?
  • HTTPS is NOT enough: A padlock icon just means the connection is encrypted. Attackers can get SSL certificates for fake domains.
  • Look for subdomains: `paypal.com.secure-login.net` actually belongs to `secure-login.net`, not PayPal.

Module 6: Social Engineering

Hackers manipulate human emotions to bypass logic. Watch out for:

Authority: "I'm the CEO, send me iTunes gift cards."
Fear: "Your PC is infected, call this number."
Curiosity: "Check out pictures from the party!"
Greed: "You won a $500 Amazon card."

Module 7: Real-World Case Studies

The $100 Million Scam (Google & Facebook)

A hacker set up a fake company pretending to be Quanta Computer, a legitimate hardware supplier. They sent forged invoices to Google and Facebook. Employees paid them, resulting in over $100 million lost.


Lesson: Always verify payment destination changes through a secondary channel (e.g., calling the supplier directly using a known good phone number).

Module 8: Cybersecurity Best Practices

  • Use a Password Manager: Never reuse passwords.
  • Enable MFA: Multi-Factor Authentication stops 99% of automated credential attacks.
  • Hover before you click: Always inspect links.
  • Keep Software Updated: Patching closes security holes.
  • Report it: Use your company's "Phish Alert" button.
